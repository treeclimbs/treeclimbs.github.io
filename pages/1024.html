Description & Commentary:

A stamped piece of sheet metal forms the bi-directional gate for this carabiner. A sliding piece sandwiches the gate and contains a pin which rides in a slot in the gate. The sliding piece is supported by two tightly coiled springs, anchored on the center of the spine. The pin of the sliding piece rests in a small indentation in the gate slot, preventing the gate from opening by pressing inwards.

If the sliding piece is moved slightly towards one end of the carabiner, the gate becomes unlocked and can be pushed open from the gate-face (such as when pressing the gate against a bolt to clip). At this point, the lack of spring pressure against the center of the gate also permits the gate to fall open slightly, depending on carabiner orientation.

Once the pin reaches the end of the slot, it forces the gate to pivot open on the rivet as the sliding piece continues to move to its furthest position. When released, the coil springs return the sliding piece to the center, forcing the gate closed and "locked."

The gate is secure (locking) in that it resists inwards pressure, but insecure in that a single action of moving the sliding piece will open the gate. In this way, this gate mechanism does not fit conventional categories of locking carabiners, much like the Grivel Tau. In use, I consider the Fifty:Fifty a "light" or "low" security carabiner like the Edelrid Slide locks - more secure than a non-locker and almost as fast to use, but not as secure as a true screw lock or more traditional autolocker.

It is unclear to me the best use case for this carabiner. If intended for use as a belay or rappel carabiner, it is on the heavy end for a fully featured locking carabiner with anti-crossload features (but not the heaviest), so the design's unique features need to be worth the weight.

The double ended design would suggest it is a good fit for use with Assistive Braking Devices, but the step down from the nose to gate can bind on the sideplates of the most common (the Petzl Grigri). A stiff cable keeper (bail) on a traditional slot-style belay device (e.g. Black Diamond ATC) can force the central sliding piece downwards when loaded, automatically opening the gate. Not great.

One application may be in cold, gloved or other low dexterity situations requiring operation with gross motor skills. Unlike some traditional auto-lockers, the large tabs on the locking mechanism are easy to operate when wearing gloves, and the design looks like it could be relatively resistant to snow and ice.

The Gibb Hook (used by sailors) employs a similar pivoting motion to unlock, easily operated while wearing gloves on cold hands, but does not unlock and open in a single action as found on the Fifty:Fifty. Palm squeeze locking mechanisms are popular with gloved users in industrial settings, but often have a closed captive eye and thus are less flexible in application - inappropriate for field use.

Overall, this is a very interesting carabiner and wonderfully satisfying to operate. I look forward to seeing if it is yet another gimmick from the explosion of ABD driven anti-crossload designs or if the bidirectional gate finds a niche in a particular rope discipline.
