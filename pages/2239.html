Description & Commentary:

The gate of Stubai Genius carabiners can be held open by a pivoting lever along the upper spine and basket. The end of the gate tucks under a small tab on the end of the lever, holding it in place until the lever is depressed.

The lever serves two functions: a) to release the gate and b) block the gate when a load is applied to the basket.

As with the Kong Ergo Wire Lock, I have noticed that if the load is applied before the gate closes, the gate will be trapped slightly open until the load is briefly removed. This is more likely to happen when I manually open the gate, and release the gate after loading the basket.

Another way this can occur is by pressing the carabiner onto the object such that the gate opens all the way and the object presses against the lever along the interior spine. Once free of the gate, the gate begins closing, but the object/load continues to depress the lever as it slides into the basket - and blocking the gate from seating fully in the notch.

Two spurs in the interior form a semi-captive eye for webbing, and the opening permits the sling/dogbone to be replaced by the user (unlike the DMM Mamba).

It is uncommon to see carabiners formed from stamped flat stock, in part because of how the rope runs over the square edges. The Genius overcomes this with the rounded lever insert and perhaps keeps production costs down for the carabiner body itself.

A notch & window (vs notch & pin) gate latch partially shields the rear of the gate. Typically a gate shield is addressing the potential abrasion/cutting of the rope if the carabiner is crossloaded, but that is highly unlikely in thes design.

The Genius A model has three grooves on the gate, and the Genius B model has a single hourglassed section in the center of the gate. The B model also has extended ears on the sides of the latching lever, which helps prevent rope from becoming stuck on the bottom of the latch as compared to the A model.
