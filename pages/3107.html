Description & Commentary:

This relatively modern Salewa carabiner uses a compact palm-squeeze lock with a unique compound locking piece. The notch & pin gate, hotforged body and laser markings would suggest a something from 2000/2010, but I have not seen Salewa's marketing for this particular model. The features and design suggest use on some sort of webbing lanyard, such as for via ferrata.

The stainless locking piece holds a sprung black plastic block which prevents the gate from opening. This mechanism uses over center geometry to block the gate from opening, but once moved slightly the gate can rotate the plastic block and open fully.

The over-center gate block means the palm squeeze mechanism requires an unusally short throw in a compact package. The gate is sprung as typical, but the plastic block spring provides additional gate return force. When opening, the gate can move far enough to disengage the notch & pin latch before it contacts the black piece, so it is unclear how useful this feature is to resist accidental opening. It could help create a larger sweet spot for slapping the carabiner onto an anchor point or cable when climbing via ferrata.

There may be other advantages to this arrangement - ideas? Reach out!

The carabiner spine has a fin under the locking piece, which can protrude through a cutout in the locking piece, and acts as a sort of travel limiter unless piece is depressed on the end closes to the carabiner basket. Gate can still be opened if locking piece is flush with the fin, but with great difficulty, as it is near center and still needs to rotate the black block fully (more than when the locking piece is fully depressed).

A pair of interior spurs form a semi-captive webbing eye.
