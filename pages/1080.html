I first learned to belay on this model of carabiner (using a Sticht plate) at a university program. I have yet to learn the model name and locking style of this carabiner, and will probably need to dig into some old print materials. (if you can help, please contact me)

Uniquely, this carabiner sports a full-autolocking twist-lock stage, followed by a manually activated secondary locking stage. The internal spring forces the sleeve to rotate into a locked position, as is found on basic double-action (single-locking) twist-lock carabiners.

To manually set the secondary lock (what we called supersafe mode), the climber allows the carabiner to close and twist into the locked position, then lifts the twist-locked sleeve until the indexing hinge pin aligns with the cutout. The rotational spring used for the twistlock will encourage the pin through the slot if the climber doesn't prevent it from doing so by overgripping the sleeve. Spring pressure moves the sleeve downwards, towards the hinge end of the gate.

To disable the secondary lock, the climber must slide the sleeve upwards (towards the carabiner nose) to align the pin again with the cutout, then rotate the sleeve to the twist lock position. Note that the opening between the two modes is in the middle of track for the index pin, minimizing any chance of accidentally bumping the carabiner sleeve out of this secondary lock position.

I have 4 examples in two different the Black Diamond stampings - in all 4 cases the plastic inner sleeve which supports the outer aluminum locking sleeve is cracked and allowing it to deform. Without the former structure, the plastic sleeve rubs against the outer aluminum sleeve resisting movement under spring pressure alone. One example works satisfactorily, but not consistantly enough that I would use it in the field.

Two examples look practically brand new (unlike the the one pictured here), and I find it surprising they do not function more smoothly.

I would very much like an example with an intact inner sleeve, but I wonder if this was an ongoing problem and contributed to the retirement of this design.
