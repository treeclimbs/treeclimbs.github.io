Description & Commentary:

To save weight, Salewa constructed these 49g carabiners from hollow tubing - not something seen today given the advancements in forging!

They formed the nose and tail of the carabiner body by smashing the tubing flat before creating the nose notch and hinge pin hole. The prominant nose helps catch a rope easier when clipping, protects against accidental opening, and indicates gate orientation at a glance.

Strength ratings are listed in "kp" or kiloponds - kilograms of force, or ~9.8 Newtons at standard gravity. Thus the 2000 kp rating is slightly more than 19.6 kN.

Unlike non-locking models, this model lacks the "tested" stamp on the gate (or elsewhere).

Alpinist magazine stated that this model of carabiner was also innovative in that each carabiner was individually tested, but Pierre Allain has been doing the same since 1965. Additionally, it appears Salewa began individual testing after a recall in 1971, but I think hollow tubing carabiners were first released in 1977.

Salewa applied for a patent in the US in 1977, approved in 1978. They advertised D-shaped carabiners in Mountain magazine in 1978 (and 1979).

Bergsport's 1986 catalog shows the locking model in aluminum and steel.

Unlike the non-locking models, the gate is also made from hollow tubing and the nose notch interfaces with a hole (window) in the gate rather than a pin. The internal locking sleeve threads follow a protruding cross pin located just below nose notch which also holds the gate spring in place. A snap ring underneath the locking sleeve acts as a stop, limiting travel before the sleeve can jam against the nose. Locking motion is quick, requring less than a full turn of rotation, but leaves the carabiner highly susceptable to accidental unlocking.

This particular example has two marks running longitudinally on the right side of the gate, from under the locking sleeve down to the hinge end of the gate. These appear to be hairline cracks and curve towards each other near the gate end. One crack runs through the hinge rivet hole(!).
