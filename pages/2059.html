Description & Commentary:

An update to the iconic original Petzl William, retaining keylock gate latch, gate relief hole, large gate opening and huge basket capacity, while reducing weight with a hot-forged H-frame. Offered in many different locking sleeves, this model uses the updated Ball Lock, a button-twist lock mechanism.

Compared to the earlier generation of metal sleeve ball lock carabiners, the button of this model protrudes slightly more, but is still wider than the original plastic sleeve version. I find it equally easy to operate as the previous metal-sleeve version.

The opposite side of the sleeve has two vertical texture bars which make the sleeve easiser to rotate, which could be a problem for the previous version in poor weather, wearing gloves or with sweaty hands. The rest of the knurling is also more aggressive. Overall, these are welcome improvements in usability.

Climbers typically depress the button with the thumb or middle finger, depending on carabiner orientation and which hand is used. If the carabiner fits the hand well, the gate unlocks more easily than a lift-and-twist (e.g. Petzl Triact), thanks to a single motion and short 45 deg rotation. The button mechanism provides better security against accidental openings than simple twist or lift-and-twist carabiners, especially Petzl's heavily textured Triact gates of this era.

The ball-lock mechanism is my preferred auto-locking mechanism for tree climbing if I do not need a steel carabiner or ANSI gate-face rated carabiner. The only downside is that Petzl only offers the Ball Lock on relatively heavy Am'D and William carabiners.

The large size of the William makes it popular as a masterpoint or belay station management carabiner, or as a "do anything" spare carabiner that can hold large objects or many smaller ones. For this reason, I equip new tree climbing students with at least 1 William - the huge basket holds multiple strands of beginner-friendly 1/2" rope as required by certain rope management or self-rescue techniques.

This model came with a sleeve which shrouds the locking button, requiring a tool to depress - similar to Petzl's Pinlock system.

The sleeve appears to be an aftermarket accessory, although it is well made and finished. The examples I have of this sleeve were acquired from the UK. If you have any information on this, please contact me.

Pinlock systems are typically found in commercial programs with novice or untrustworthy users such as adventure parks, zip tours, ropes courses, or climbing gyms (think kids birthday parties).
